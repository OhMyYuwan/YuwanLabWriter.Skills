# Resilience Playbook

## First Response

Use concrete recognition:

```text
This sounds exhausting in a very specific way: you have invested enough that stopping feels costly, but you are not yet getting evidence that the path works.
```

Then ask one grounding question if needed:

```text
Are you still able to sleep/eat/work at a basic level, or has this started to affect daily functioning?
```

## Common Patterns

### Mid-PhD Valley

Signals: "third/fourth year", "nothing to show", "everyone else has papers", "I may not graduate".

Response:

- Normalize the structure, not the suffering.
- Convert "nothing" into a factual inventory: papers read, experiments run, skills learned, tools built, people consulted.
- Ask for the actual graduation distance: required papers, committee expectations, remaining experiments.

### Comparison Spiral

Response:

- Explain that doctoral projects are not comparable by visible outputs alone.
- Ask what metric would show progress relative to the user's past self.
- Reduce exposure to social feeds or lab rumor channels if they worsen functioning.

### Toxic Supervision

Signals: public humiliation, threats around graduation/visa, stolen credit, unreasonable non-research demands, physical symptoms before meetings.

Response:

- Validate that this is structural, not a mindset flaw.
- Recommend written records: date, place, event, witnesses, documents.
- Identify formal and informal supports: co-advisor, committee, department graduate office, ombuds, trusted faculty.
- Do not urge confrontation without a safety plan.

### Wanting to Quit

First distinguish:

- Acute impulse after a triggering event.
- Sustained, evaluated decision after weeks or months.

Decision dimensions:

- Health and safety.
- Funding, visa, housing, family obligations.
- Transfer, leave, master-out, advisor change, project change.
- Skills and network already gained.
- What the user would move toward, not only away from.

### Project Despair

If distress comes from a stuck project, use Pang Tianyu's stop-loss rule:

1. Is the hard point solvable with current resources?
2. Is the idea still promising enough?
3. What deadline forces a continue/pivot decision?

## Stabilizing Tools

- Leave the desk for 10-20 minutes before making irreversible decisions.
- Write a two-column note: "facts" vs. "fears".
- Contact one human who knows the local context.
- Define one small action for today that is not career-defining.

## Escalation

When the user reports self-harm thoughts, imminent danger, severe inability to function, or persistent symptoms, prioritize professional and local help. Encourage contacting emergency services, a crisis hotline, university counseling, a physician, or a trusted person nearby. For current hotline numbers or policies, verify current local sources.
