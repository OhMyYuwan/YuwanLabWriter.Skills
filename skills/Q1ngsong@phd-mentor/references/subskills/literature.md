# Literature Protocol

## Three Reading Depths

### Screen

Goal: decide whether the paper matters to the user's current problem.

Use five questions:

1. What is this paper about?
2. What problem does it solve?
3. Why is it interesting?
4. What is genuinely new?
5. Why is it elegant or useful?

Time budget: 5-10 minutes.

### Read

Goal: understand the claim, method, evidence, and limits.

Read abstract, introduction, figures, method overview, experiments, limitations, conclusion. Write a short note in your own words.

Time budget: 1-2 hours.

### Study

Goal: internalize enough to reproduce, critique, teach, or extend the work.

Actions: rederive, implement, trace references, inspect assumptions, compare baselines, and write what future work follows.

Time budget: one day to one week.

## Four Reader Stages

- **Passive**: can summarize the paper.
- **Active**: can use parts of the paper.
- **Critical**: can locate assumptions, weaknesses, and boundary conditions.
- **Creative**: can generate a next question from the paper.

Ask the user which stage they are at before prescribing tactics.

## Ten-Question Deep Note

1. What exact problem is the paper solving?
2. Is the problem new, newly important, or newly solvable?
3. What assumptions does the paper rely on?
4. What related works are central?
5. What is the core solution and why is it different?
6. Does the experiment design test the main claim?
7. What data, benchmark, code, or measurement setup matters?
8. Do the results support the claim? Where are the boundaries?
9. What is the contribution in one sentence?
10. What could be done next?

For beginners, focus on 1, 2, 5, 9. For active researchers, focus on 4, 6, 7, 8, 10.

## Field Map Method

Use when the user is writing a survey, related work, or entering a new area.

```text
Problem type | Core assumption | Method family | Representative papers | Evidence type | Limitation | Next opening
```

Steps:

1. Start with 2-3 recent surveys or tutorials.
2. Add 20-40 closely related papers.
3. Cluster by the problem each paper answers.
4. Identify the dominant assumptions and avoided cases.
5. Select 5-10 anchor papers to study deeply.
6. Turn limitations into candidate research questions.

## Filter Strategy

Do not try to "follow everything."

- Track 3-5 core groups or authors.
- Track 2-3 venues that define the user's field.
- Set keyword alerts for the current problem, not the whole discipline.
- Use conference programs and table-of-contents alerts for periodic review.
