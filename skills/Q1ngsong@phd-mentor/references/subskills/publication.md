# Publication Playbook

## Paper Story Arc

Use this before section-level editing:

1. **Why**: why should anyone care?
2. **Gap**: what is missing or wrong in existing work?
3. **Claim**: what do we show, prove, build, or reveal?
4. **Evidence**: what data, experiment, proof, or analysis supports the claim?
5. **Boundary**: when does it fail or not apply?
6. **Consequence**: what changes for the reader?

If the user cannot state the claim in one sentence, do not polish prose yet.

## First-Pass Surfaces

Reviewers and editors often decide whether a manuscript deserves careful attention from:

- Title
- Abstract
- Introduction first page
- Contribution list
- Key figures and legends
- Conclusion

Make these surfaces carry the paper's story without needing the full method section.

## Section Guidance

### Title

Avoid filler: "Study on", "Research on", "Investigation of".

Prefer: object + contribution/mechanism + context.

### Abstract

Answer: problem, method, result, conclusion. Do not add information absent from the paper.

### Introduction

Use: known -> unknown -> why it matters -> what we do -> what we found -> why it changes the field.

Contribution lists should contain a few sharp contributions, not many shallow ones.

### Methods

A competent peer should be able to reproduce the work. Omit performance theatre; include enough detail to trust the result.

### Results

Organize by claims, not by chronology. Each table or figure should answer a question.

### Discussion

Start with the answer, then explain, compare, defend limitations, and zoom out.

## Venue Fit

Check:

- Scope match with recent accepted papers.
- Review cycle vs. graduation timeline.
- Community norms: conference vs. journal, double blind, rebuttal, supplementary material.
- Evidence standard: proof, benchmark, real-world deployment, qualitative analysis, or mechanistic explanation.

## Rejection Handling

Wait before rewriting. Then classify:

- Desk reject: scope, format, anonymity, or obvious positioning problem.
- Weak reject / revise invitation: usually worth a substantial revision.
- Split reviews: story and claim may be unclear.
- Consistent strong reject: revise the work, not just the cover letter.

Use `paper-revise` when comments must be answered point by point.

## Cover Letter Shape

```text
Dear [Editor],

We submit [title] for consideration in [journal].

This manuscript addresses [problem]. We show [main finding] using [method/evidence].

Its significance is [why journal readers should care].

The work is original, not under consideration elsewhere, and all authors approve submission.
```
