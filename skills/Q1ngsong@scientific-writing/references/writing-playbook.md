# Writing Playbook

## Diagnose Before Editing

Ask what kind of problem the draft has:

- Buried claim.
- Weak section logic.
- Paragraph with two ideas.
- Sentence clutter.
- Grammar or terminology errors.
- Overclaiming beyond evidence.

Do not start with grammar if the claim is unclear.

## Reader Expectation Principles

From Gopen and Swan:

- Keep subject and verb close when possible.
- Put context/old information before new information.
- Use sentence endings for emphasis.
- Use structure to guide interpretation, not just to decorate prose.

## Story-First Drafting

Before writing a full paper:

```text
One-sentence claim:
Key figure/table:
What the figure proves:
Reader objection:
Evidence still missing:
```

This follows the idea that writing can clarify the research, not merely report completed research.

## Section Shapes

### Introduction

Known -> gap -> why it matters -> approach -> result -> contribution.

### Methods

Enough detail for a competent peer to reproduce or evaluate trustworthiness.

### Results

Organize by claims. Do not narrate experiment chronology unless chronology is the finding.

### Discussion

Answer the question first, then interpret, compare, limit, and zoom out.

### Abstract

Problem -> method -> result -> conclusion. Keep claims within the manuscript's evidence.

## Revision Passes

1. **Structure pass**: Does each section do its job?
2. **Paragraph pass**: Does each paragraph have one point and a clear topic sentence?
3. **Sentence pass**: Cut clutter, strengthen verbs, reduce subject-verb distance.
4. **Final pass**: numbers, references, figure/table consistency, terminology.

## Before/After Format

Use this when editing user text:

```text
Original:
[text]

Revision:
[text]

What changed:
- [specific reason]
```
